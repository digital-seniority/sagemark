Choosing a community

Choosing Memory Care in Mount Vernon & the Skagit Valley: 12 Questions to Ask on a Tour

When it's time to tour, the right questions turn a polished lobby into a place you can actually understand. Here are the twelve to bring with you — and how to read the answers like a family who knows what matters.

Whispering Willows Care Team
Reviewed by Anneline Breetzke, Senior Care Specialist · Updated June 2026 · 8 min read
⚡ Quick answer

The best way to choose memory care is to tour more than one community and ask the same 12 questions at each — about staff-to-resident ratios, dementia training, staff turnover, safety, personalized care plans, and what happens as needs progress. Trust what you observe as much as what you're told, and visit twice, including an unannounced or mealtime visit.

By the time a family is touring memory care, the hardest decisions are usually behind them. You've noticed the changes, talked with a doctor, and accepted that home may no longer be enough. What's left is a different kind of question — not whether, but where. And here, walking through one community after another, it is surprisingly easy to be swayed by the wrong things: a pretty chandelier, a fresh coat of paint, a warm host who knows exactly what to say.

This guide is built to keep you grounded in what actually matters. The centerpiece is a list of twelve questions to ask on every tour — questions that get past the lobby and into the daily life your parent would really live. You can print them, bring them, and use the same dozen at each place you visit, so you're comparing communities on the same terms. You know your parent better than anyone. These questions simply help you ask out loud what you already care about.

A quick note on scale, because families always wonder: across the country, an estimated 7.2 million Americans age 65 and older are living with Alzheimer's dementia, and the great majority will need supportive care at some point.3 Communities like the ones you'll tour exist precisely for this moment — and the good ones welcome every hard question you can bring.

Key takeaways

  • Trust what you observe on a tour as much as what you're told — the feel of a place rarely lies.
  • Continuity of staff and genuinely person-centered care are the two biggest signals of memory-care quality.
  • Ask what happens as needs progress, so a good fit today doesn't become a disruptive move later.
  • A confident, caring community welcomes hard questions; pressure to sign quickly or vague answers are red flags.

The 12 questions to ask on a memory care tour

Bring this list to every community you visit. Under each question is why it matters and what a strong, honest answer tends to sound like — so you can listen not just for a "yes," but for the specifics behind it.

Print this and bring it on every tour

  1. What is your staff-to-resident ratio — daytime and overnight? Attention and safety come down to how many caring hands are actually on the floor. Listen for a specific number, and for honesty about nights and early mornings, when staffing is often thinnest. "It depends" without any figure is a soft answer; "Here's our daytime ratio, and here's what overnight looks like" is a confident one.
  2. How are staff trained specifically in dementia care, and how often? Memory care is not the same as general caregiving — it calls for specialized skills in communication, redirection, and supporting people through confusion. You want to hear about dedicated dementia training that is ongoing, not a single orientation video on day one.
  3. What is your staff turnover, and how long have your caregivers been here? For someone with memory loss, a familiar face is a form of safety. Continuity matters enormously — meeting caregivers who have been there for years is one of the most reassuring things you can see. High churn, even in a beautiful building, is hard to overcome.
  4. How do you keep residents safe from wandering and falls? You want thoughtful safety — secured entries and exits, enclosed gardens residents can actually enjoy, sensible monitoring — without the place feeling like a lockdown. A good answer describes safety that protects dignity and still lets people move, walk, and go outside.
  5. How do you build a personalized care plan, and how often is it updated? Good memory care starts from the person, not a template. Listen for how they learn your parent's life history, preferences, and routines, who is involved in the plan, and how regularly it's revisited as things change — a real plan is a living document, not a form in a binder.
  6. What happens as my parent's needs change — can they stay as the disease progresses? Dementia changes over time, and another move can be deeply disruptive. Ask whether residents can "age in place" as care needs increase, and what specifically would trigger a transition elsewhere. Knowing this now spares your family a wrenching surprise later.
  7. What does a typical day look like? A meaningful day has shape — purpose, gentle structure, and engagement suited to where someone is. Ask to see an activity calendar and, better yet, to watch a moment of real life. You're listening for music, movement, small jobs, and connection — not a television murmuring in an empty day room.
  8. How is dining handled for someone with dementia? Eating and drinking get complicated with memory loss. Strong programs adapt: finger foods when utensils get hard, gentle cueing and company at the table, attention to hydration, and flexibility for the appetite changes that come and go. Ask whether mealtimes feel calm and unhurried.
  9. How do you coordinate medical care and medications? Find out who manages medications, whether providers visit on-site or residents go out, and how the community keeps your parent's own doctor in the loop. You want clear medication management and a team that communicates with the family physician rather than working in a silo.
  10. How — and how often — will you communicate with our family? The best communities reach out before you have to ask. Listen for proactive, regular updates — a portal, scheduled check-ins, a real person you can call — not just a phone call when something has already gone wrong.
  11. How do you handle behavioral changes, agitation, or a difficult day — without over-relying on medication? This is one of the clearest quality signals in all of memory care. A strong answer leads with calm, redirection, knowing the person, and adjusting the environment — using medication thoughtfully and sparingly, never as the first and only tool when someone is having a hard day.
  12. What are all the costs, and what would trigger a price increase? Ask for everything: the base rate, care-tier add-ons that rise with need, the community or move-in fee, and exactly what would cause the monthly price to go up. Transparency now prevents painful surprises later. (Our companion guide to memory care costs in Washington walks through how these pieces typically fit together.)

Person-centered care: the idea behind the questions

If you look closely, most of those twelve questions are circling the same idea. Good memory care doesn't start with the diagnosis — it starts with the person. Who were they? A gardener, a teacher, a night-shift nurse, a churchgoer, someone who took their coffee a particular way and disliked being rushed. Person-centered care means building the day around that history, those habits, and those small joys, so that even as memory fades, dignity and routine are protected.

This isn't a soft nicety; it's the approach that dementia-care experts encourage. The Alzheimer's Association and the National Institute on Aging both emphasize care that is individualized — built around a person's life story, preferences, and remaining strengths rather than a one-size-fits-all schedule.1,2 When you ask how a community writes its care plans, how it learns who your parent is, and how it handles a hard day, you're really asking one question: will my mother, my father, be known here?

Green flags and red flags on a tour

Some signals are easy to read once you know to look for them. As you walk through, keep a quiet mental tally. None of these is a single verdict, but the balance you feel by the end of a visit tells you a great deal.

✦ Trust your senses on a tour

Before you weigh any answer, notice how the place feels. What does it sound like — laughter and conversation, or silence and a blaring television? How do staff speak to residents when they think no one is grading them: with patience and warmth, or briskly? Do residents look engaged and cared for? Then go back. Visit twice if you can, and make one of those visits unannounced or at mealtime — the truest picture of a community is the ordinary Tuesday you weren't expected to see.

The right community feels less like a facility and more like a home — a place where your parent is known, not just looked after.

A note for Skagit Valley families

There's a particular comfort in choosing close to home. When a community is right here in Mount Vernon or nearby in the Skagit Valley, you can visit often — a weekday lunch, a Sunday afternoon, a quick stop on the way home — and showing up easily, again and again, is one of the most powerful things a family can offer. There's value, too, in a community that knows this place: the rhythms of the valley, the local doctors and hospitals, the tulip fields in spring that so many of our residents remember. Touring nearby also makes it simple to do the thing we most recommend — visit more than once, and compare two or three communities side by side before you decide.

Frequently asked questions

What should I look for in a memory care community? +

Look at the people and the feel of the place as much as the building. The strongest quality signals are continuity of staff (low turnover and caregivers who know residents by name), specialized and ongoing dementia training, a person-centered care plan built around your parent's history and routines, and a calm, home-like atmosphere that is secured without feeling like a lockdown. Ask how the community handles a difficult day without over-relying on medication, and how it will communicate with your family. Trust what you observe on the tour, and visit more than once before deciding.

How many memory care communities are in the Mount Vernon area? +

There are multiple memory care and assisted-living communities serving Mount Vernon and the wider Skagit County area, ranging from small, home-like settings to larger senior-living campuses. Rather than relying on a single count, plan to tour more than one in person — the right fit depends far more on staffing, training, philosophy of care, and how the place feels to your family than on the number of options. Washington's Department of Social and Health Services licenses these communities, and touring two or three side by side is the best way to compare.

What's the difference between memory care and a nursing home? +

Memory care is a residential, dementia-specialized form of long-term care: it supports daily living and meaningful engagement in a secured, home-like setting, with staff trained specifically for Alzheimer's and other dementias. A nursing home (skilled nursing facility) provides higher-acuity, around-the-clock medical and skilled nursing care for people with significant medical or rehabilitation needs. Many people with dementia are well served by memory care for years; skilled nursing becomes relevant when complex medical needs require that level of clinical support.

How do I know if it's time for memory care? +

Common signs include safety problems at home (wandering, leaving the stove on, falls, getting lost), needs that have outgrown what family can safely provide, increasing help required with daily tasks like bathing, dressing, and medications, and caregiver exhaustion or burnout. It is rarely a single moment — it is usually a pattern that builds over time. If you are still sorting out whether the changes you are seeing are part of dementia at all, our guide to the early signs of dementia is a good place to start.

Should I involve my parent in choosing? +

Yes, wherever possible. Include your parent in the decision as much as their stage of dementia allows — their preferences about location, daily routine, food, and the feel of a place still matter, and being part of the choice supports dignity and a smoother transition. In earlier stages they may tour with you and weigh in directly; in later stages you might involve them in smaller, manageable ways while you carry more of the decision. The goal is to honor who they are, not to test them.
