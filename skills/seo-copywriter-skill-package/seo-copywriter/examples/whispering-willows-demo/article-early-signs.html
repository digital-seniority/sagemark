Recognizing the signs

The 10 Early Signs of Dementia in an Aging Parent

It usually starts as a quiet worry — a repeated question, a missed bill, a wrong turn on a familiar road. Here is how to tell ordinary aging apart from a warning sign, calmly and without panic.

Whispering Willows Care Team
Reviewed by Anneline Breetzke, Senior Care Specialist · Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
⚡ Quick answer

The Alzheimer's Association lists 10 early warning signs of dementia — including memory loss that disrupts daily life, trouble planning or solving problems, difficulty with familiar tasks, confusion with time or place, and changes in mood or judgment. What separates them from normal aging is that the changes persist, worsen over time, and interfere with everyday independence. If you notice several, see a doctor — some causes are treatable.

If you are reading this, you have probably already felt it: a small, persistent sense that something with Mom or Dad is not quite the same. Maybe she told the same story twice in one visit. Maybe he seemed lost paying at the register. It is one of the most unsettling feelings an adult child can have — and one of the most common.

You are far from alone. An estimated 7.2 million Americans age 65 and older are living with Alzheimer's dementia — and in 2025, for the first time, that number passed seven million.1 The great majority are cared for, at least at first, by family who noticed the early changes and weren't sure what to do.

This guide is built around the Alzheimer's Association's 10 warning signs, the most widely trusted checklist clinicians point families to.2 For each one, we put the warning sign next to the ordinary age-related change it is so easily confused with — because knowing the difference is what turns worry into a clear next step.

Key takeaways

  • Occasional forgetfulness is a normal part of aging; the warning sign is when memory or thinking changes disrupt daily life and don't resolve.
  • Look for a pattern over time, not a single bad moment — frequency and progression matter more than any one slip.
  • Several reversible conditions can mimic dementia, which is exactly why an early doctor's visit is so valuable.
  • An early evaluation buys what families want most: answers, more treatment options, and time to plan together.

Healthy older brains do slow down a little. The table below pairs each of the Alzheimer's Association's ten warning signs with the everyday change it should not be mistaken for. If you recognize several of the left-hand column happening regularly, it is worth a conversation with a doctor.

⌖ The pattern is the point

No single moment on this list means dementia. Tired, stressed, grieving, dehydrated, or over-medicated people forget things too. What matters is a cluster of changes that builds over weeks and months and starts to interfere with everyday independence.

Why an early conversation with a doctor matters so much

Families often wait — out of fear, out of respect for a parent's pride, out of hope that it's "just age." It's a deeply human instinct. But waiting rarely helps, and an early visit can change everything for two reasons.

First, some causes are reversible

This is the part too few families know. A number of treatable conditions can look almost exactly like early dementia: thyroid imbalances, a vitamin B12 deficiency, depression, urinary or other infections, sleep disorders, and interactions between medications. The National Institute on Aging encourages an evaluation precisely so these can be identified and treated.3 Sometimes the "memory problem" resolves once the real cause is addressed.

Second, if it is dementia, early is better

When the cause is Alzheimer's or another dementia, an early diagnosis gives a family room to breathe. It can mean access to treatments that work best when started early, the chance to put legal and financial plans in place while your parent can still take part, and time — time to learn, to find support, and to make decisions together rather than in a crisis.

An early diagnosis doesn't take away time. It gives it back — as understanding, as choices, and as the chance to plan side by side.

How to start the conversation

Bringing it up is often harder than noticing the signs. Your parent may feel frightened or defensive — that, too, is normal. A few approaches tend to help.

A gentle way in

  1. Pick a calm, private moment. Not at a busy holiday table, not in front of grandchildren — somewhere unhurried and familiar.
  2. Lead with love, not a list of mistakes. "I love you and I want you to feel your best" lands very differently than a catalog of recent errors.
  3. Use "I" statements. "I've noticed I'm repeating things to you and I want to help" feels like partnership, not accusation.
  4. Make the doctor visit routine. Frame it as a general check-up for overall health rather than a "memory test."
  5. Start with a trusted face. The family doctor your parent already knows is often the easiest first door.
  6. Bring specifics, quietly. Jot down concrete examples and a current medication list to share with the clinician — not to read aloud to your parent.
⚑ When to seek help right away

Some changes shouldn't wait for a scheduled appointment. A sudden confusion, a rapid change over hours or days, getting lost while driving, leaving the stove on, wandering from home, or any situation where your parent's immediate safety is at risk warrants prompt medical attention.

What comes after "what's wrong?"

If an evaluation points toward dementia, the natural next questions are practical ones: how do we keep them safe, how do we afford good care, and how do we choose the right place if home is no longer enough? You don't have to answer them today — but you don't have to face them blind, either. Frequently asked questions

What is usually the very first sign of dementia? +

The most common first sign is short-term memory loss that disrupts daily life — forgetting recently learned information, asking the same question repeatedly, or leaning heavily on notes and family for things once handled independently. Occasionally forgetting a name and remembering it later is typical aging; the warning sign is forgetting more often and not recalling it later.

How is normal forgetfulness different from dementia? +

Normal aging looks like misplacing keys, missing a payment, or briefly blanking on a word — and then catching it. Dementia warning signs are more disruptive and persistent: getting lost on a familiar route, struggling to follow a long-used recipe, losing track of the season, or being unable to retrace steps. Pattern and frequency matter more than any single moment.

Should I see a doctor for early signs, or wait? +

Sooner is better. Several reversible conditions — thyroid problems, B12 deficiency, medication interactions, depression, infections — can mimic dementia, and an early evaluation can rule them in or out. If the cause is a dementia, early diagnosis opens more treatment options, time to plan, and access to support.

How do I talk to my parent without upsetting them? +

Choose a calm, private moment, lead with love rather than a list of mistakes, and use "I" statements instead of "you" accusations. Frame a doctor's visit as a routine check-up. Expect some defensiveness — it's normal — and it's often easiest to start with the family doctor your parent already trusts.

Does having early signs mean it's definitely Alzheimer's? +

No. These signs warrant a medical evaluation, not a self-diagnosis. Alzheimer's is the most common cause of dementia, but symptoms can also come from other dementias or from treatable conditions. Only a qualified clinician can determine the cause.
