What does memory care include?

Before the question of how to pay, it helps to understand what you're paying for — because the value of memory care is the whole package of safety, expertise, and daily life it provides. A memory care monthly fee typically bundles a generous set of services into one base figure, while certain needs are billed on top as they arise. The list below shows the general shape of what's usually included. This is industry structure, not a Whispering Willows fee sheet; every community draws the lines a little differently, so ask each one to walk you through its own.

What a monthly fee typically includes

  1. A private or shared suite. A comfortable room your loved one can settle into and make their own.
  2. Meals and snacks. Nutritious meals each day, with help and gentle prompting at the table as needed.
  3. 24-hour dementia-trained staffing. Around-the-clock care from a team trained specifically for memory loss.
  4. A secured environment. A setting designed to keep residents who tend to wander safe, with freedom to move within it.
  5. Housekeeping and laundry. Day-to-day upkeep so families can simply visit and enjoy their time together.
  6. Life-enrichment activities. A daily rhythm of music, movement, and connection built around people living with dementia.
  7. Basic personal-care assistance. Gentle help with bathing, dressing, grooming, and the everyday tasks of living.

When you compare communities, compare the all-in picture — what's included in the base fee and what may be billed separately as care needs change. That's also why a single advertised number rarely tells the whole story, and why a short conversation gives you a clearer answer than a web page can. If you're still weighing memory care against a less specialized setting, our guide to memory care vs. assisted living breaks down the difference.

What are the ways families pay for memory care?

Here is the reassuring part. Very few families write a single check from one account. Most assemble the monthly fee from a combination of the sources below — and some of these, like Veterans benefits, are missed simply because no one mentions them. Read these as options to explore, not advice; your own eligibility depends on your specific situation.

Common ways families pay

  1. Private pay. Savings, pensions, Social Security, and retirement accounts — the most direct source for many families.
  2. Proceeds from selling or renting the family home. Often the largest single source; selling frees the equity, while renting can create monthly income to offset care.
  3. Long-term care insurance. If a policy is already in place, check whether memory care qualifies and review the elimination period (the waiting time before benefits begin).
  4. Veterans benefits — VA Aid & Attendance. This pension can help eligible wartime veterans and their surviving spouses pay for care.
  5. Medicaid — Washington Apple Health. For those who meet the income and asset limits, it can help cover long-term care. Whispering Willows accepts Medicaid without a spend-down.
  6. Life insurance conversions. Some policies can be converted into a long-term-care benefit that pays toward care while your loved one is living.
  7. Bridge loans or elder-care lines of credit. Short-term financing that covers care while a home sells or benefits begin, then is repaid once those funds arrive.
✓ A note on eligibility

Benefits programs each have their own rules, and they change. Before counting on any source above, verify your family's eligibility with the appropriate authority — the VA for Aid & Attendance, the Washington Health Care Authority for Apple Health (Medicaid), or a qualified benefits advisor or elder-law professional. Think of this as "talk to a professional," not financial advice — the right guide can often uncover help you didn't know you qualified for.

Does Medicare pay for memory care?

No — and this is the single most misunderstood point about paying for care, so let's be clear. Medicare does not cover long-term "custodial" memory care — the ongoing help with bathing, dressing, eating, and daily living that a residential memory care community provides. Medicare may cover short, medically necessary skilled care — for example, a limited skilled-nursing or rehabilitation stay after a hospitalization — but it does not pay for ongoing room-and-board memory care.1

⚑ Important: Medicare does not pay for long-term memory care

Don't assume Medicare simply "covers" memory care — it doesn't cover the long-term, day-to-day custodial care that defines it. Medicare's role is limited to brief, skilled medical care, often after a hospital stay. Plan your funding around the other sources on this page, and let our team help you map the ones that fit your situation.

Does Medicaid (Apple Health) cover memory care in Washington?

Yes, for those who qualify. Washington's Medicaid program, called Apple Health, can help cover long-term care for people who meet the income and asset limits.2 Because eligibility rules and the availability of Medicaid beds vary by community — and not every community accepts Medicaid — the best way to know what applies to your family is to verify your situation with the Washington Health Care Authority or a benefits advisor, and to ask each community directly whether it accepts Medicaid. That last question matters, because the answer is not the same everywhere.
