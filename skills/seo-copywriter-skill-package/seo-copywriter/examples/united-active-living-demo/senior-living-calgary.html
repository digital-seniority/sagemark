What does "senior living" actually mean in Calgary?

"Senior living" isn't one thing — it's an umbrella over a range of homes and services designed for older adults, from those who simply want a maintenance-free lifestyle to those who need around-the-clock specialized care. The reason it feels confusing is that families, brochures, and government programs all use slightly different words for overlapping ideas. In everyday language, people say retirement home, seniors' residence, or assisted living; in Alberta's official system, the same landscape is described as supportive living and continuing care. Both are talking about the same goal: the right amount of support, in the right setting, as needs change.

This matters now more than it used to. Canada's population is aging quickly — as of the 2021 Census, more than 1 in 5 Canadians (about 19 percent) were aged 65 or older, the highest share ever recorded, and the senior population continues to grow faster than the population as a whole.1 Alberta is no exception, and Calgary families are increasingly facing this decision earlier and with more options than their own parents had. Understanding the vocabulary is the first step to feeling in control of it.

ℹ A quick translation

When you hear "retirement home" or "independent living," think private-pay lifestyle with services. When you hear "supportive living" or "continuing care," think Alberta's care system, with publicly funded options accessed through an assessment. Many communities — United Active Living among them — blend several of these under one roof, so a resident can receive more care over time without moving homes.

What are the levels of care — independent, assisted, integrated, and memory care?

The clearest way to make sense of senior living is by level of care — how much daily support a person needs. Here is the short version of each; for a fuller side-by-side, including where Alberta's supportive living levels fit, see our companion guide on independent, assisted, and memory care in Alberta.

The levels of care, briefly

  1. Independent living. For active, largely self-sufficient seniors who want freedom from home maintenance — with meals, housekeeping, social life, and activities included, and care available if needs change.
  2. Assisted living. Adds daily personal-care support — help with bathing, dressing, medications, and mobility — for those who need a hand with the tasks of everyday living but not constant nursing.
  3. Integrated living. A blended model where independent and assisted living share one community, so a resident can receive more support over time without moving — a hallmark of how United Active Living is built.
  4. Memory care. A secure, specialized setting for people living with Alzheimer's or another dementia, with staff trained in dementia care and a daily rhythm designed to keep residents calm, safe, and engaged.

Most families don't fit neatly into one box, and that's normal — needs shift, and a good community is built to shift with them. If your parent is doing well today but you're planning ahead, integrated and memory care options matter precisely because they prevent another disruptive move later.

How does Alberta's system work — private pay vs publicly funded?

This is the part that surprises most families, because Alberta's system is different from what you may have read online — and very different from the United States. Here it is plainly. There are two broad ways senior living is paid for in Alberta.

Private retirement and independent living — and much private assisted living, which is where most of United Active Living's communities sit — are private pay. You choose the community, you tour it, and you pay a monthly fee directly, the way you would for any housing-plus-services arrangement.2

Alberta also operates a publicly funded continuing care system. In 2024 Alberta modernized it into Type A and Type B continuing care homes (Type B was formerly called Designated Supportive Living, or DSL). In these homes, health care goods and services are publicly funded at no cost to the resident; what residents pay is an accommodation charge for their room, meals, and recreation — and the maximum for that charge is set by the Government of Alberta.3 Within supportive living, levels run from SL1 (apartment-style living for fairly independent seniors) through SL4 and dementia care (24-hour on-site nursing).2

✓ How publicly funded care is accessed

You don't apply for publicly funded Type B / Designated Supportive Living the way you book a private suite. It's accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment that determines the level of care a person needs and matches them to an appropriate home.2 A good first step for the publicly funded path is to call Health Link at 811 or speak with your family physician, who can start the referral.

One more reassurance about affordability: as of January 2026, residents who are eligible for seniors' financial benefits are assured at least $373 per month in disposable income after the accommodation charge — a floor designed so that paying for accommodation doesn't leave someone with nothing.3 Families typically draw on private savings and pensions, the sale or rental of a home, and income-tested help like the Alberta Seniors Benefit. We cover all of this in detail in the cost guide.

What does it cost?

The honest answer is that it depends — on the community, the suite, and the level of care — so a single advertised number rarely tells the real story. As a market reference, United Active Living publishes suite pricing roughly in the range of $2,900 to $9,100 per month, varying by community, suite size, and care.4 Calgary memory care, across the market, is commonly estimated at roughly $3,500 to $8,000 per month.5 Treat these as ranges to orient you, not quotes.

Because the right figure is personal, the most useful next step is a short call for a tailored quote. Our full breakdown — what a monthly fee includes, how families pay, and how Alberta's publicly funded options factor in — lives in The Cost of Senior Living in Calgary.

How do I know it's time?

This is the question families wrestle with most, often quietly and for months. It's rarely a single dramatic event. More often it's a pattern that builds: more frequent falls or unsteadiness, missed medications, weight loss or meals being skipped, growing isolation, a home that's harder to keep up, mail and bills piling up — and, just as importantly, a family caregiver who is worn thin. When several of these appear together, it's usually time to start looking, before a crisis forces a rushed decision.

You don't have to judge this alone. Our guide on the signs it's time for assisted living or memory care walks through the pattern in detail with a quick checklist, and a calm conversation with a community — or with Alberta Health Services — can help you see it clearly.

The goal isn't to wait until you're certain. It's to look early enough that you get to choose calmly, rather than decide in a hurry after a fall.

How do I choose and tour a community?

Once you know roughly what level of care fits, choosing well comes down to seeing communities in person and asking the right questions. A beautiful lobby tells you very little; how the staff speak to residents, what a regular afternoon actually looks like, and how care is adjusted over time tell you almost everything. Tour more than one, bring your parent if you can, and trust how the place feels as much as the brochure.

We've put the specifics into two companion resources: Choosing a Community — the questions to ask on a tour, and a free, printable tour checklist you can bring with you to compare communities fairly, side by side.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between independent living, assisted living, and memory care? +

Independent living is for seniors who are largely self-sufficient and want a maintenance-free lifestyle with meals, activities, and community. Assisted living adds daily personal-care support, such as help with bathing, dressing, and medications. Memory care is a secure, specialized setting for people living with Alzheimer's or another dementia, staffed by a team trained in dementia care. Many Calgary communities, including United Active Living, also offer integrated living, where these levels share one building so a resident can receive more support without moving homes.

Is senior living in Calgary publicly funded or private pay? +

It depends on the type of home. Private retirement and independent living, and much private assisted living, are private pay. Alberta's publicly funded continuing care — Type A and Type B continuing care homes — covers health care goods and services at no cost, while residents pay an accommodation charge for room, meals, and recreation that is capped by the Government of Alberta. Publicly funded supportive living is accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment, not by applying directly.

How much does senior living cost in Calgary? +

Costs vary widely by community, suite size, and level of care. United Active Living publishes suite pricing roughly in the range of $2,900 to $9,100 per month, and Calgary memory care market estimates run roughly $3,500 to $8,000 per month. Because the right figure depends on the suite and the care your family member needs, the honest answer is to call for a personalized quote rather than rely on a single advertised number.

How do I know when it's time for senior living? +

It is rarely one moment. The signs usually arrive as a pattern — increasing falls or unsteadiness, missed medications, weight loss or skipped meals, isolation, a home that is harder to keep up, or a caregiver who is exhausted. When several of these appear together, it is often time to look at options. A short conversation with a community or with Alberta Health Services can help you decide.

Does United Active Living offer memory care in Calgary? +

Yes. United Active Living offers memory care through its United Minds approach across its Calgary communities, alongside independent living, assisted living, and integrated living. The communities are built around wellness, creative expression through arts and music, and lifelong learning, so residents living with dementia stay engaged in daily life.
