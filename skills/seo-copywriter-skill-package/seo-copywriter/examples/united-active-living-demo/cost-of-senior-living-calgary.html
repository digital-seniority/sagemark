The Cost of Senior Living in Calgary (2026): What's Included & How Families Pay

The question that stalls most families is "what does it cost?" — and the honest answer has two parts. First, what a monthly fee actually buys. Second, how Alberta families pay for it: private funds, the sale of a home, and Alberta's publicly funded continuing care. This guide walks through both, with real ranges and real sources — and for a figure tailored to your family, our Calgary team is one call away.

What does a monthly fee include?

Before the question of how to pay, it helps to understand what you're paying for — because most of the value of senior living lives inside the monthly fee, not on top of it. In Calgary, a senior-living monthly fee typically bundles a generous set of services into one base figure, with certain care needs billed separately as they arise. The list below shows the general shape of what's usually included. This is the industry structure, not a United Active Living fee sheet; every community draws the lines a little differently, so ask each one to walk you through its own.

What a monthly fee typically includes

  1. A private suite. A comfortable apartment your parent can settle into and make their own.
  2. Meals and snacks. Chef-prepared meals each day in a shared dining room, with help at the table as needed.
  3. Housekeeping and linen service. Regular cleaning and laundry, so the day is for living, not chores.
  4. Utilities and maintenance. Heat, water, and building upkeep — and most communities include things like a basic phone or cable package.
  5. Wellness, arts & lifelong learning. A full calendar of fitness, music, creative expression, and learning — the heart of what makes a community a home.
  6. 24-hour staffing and emergency response. Someone on site around the clock, with a way to call for help from every suite.
  7. A base level of care or care coordination. Wellness checks and help arranging care; assisted living and memory care add hands-on personal care and medication support as needs grow.

When you compare communities, compare the all-in picture — what's in the base fee and what's billed separately as care needs change. That's also why a single advertised number rarely tells the whole story, and why a short conversation gives you a clearer answer than a web page can. If you're still weighing which level of care your parent needs, our guide comparing independent, assisted, and memory care in Alberta breaks it down.

What does senior living actually cost in Calgary?

Here is the honest version of the price question. The cost of senior living in Calgary varies widely by community, suite size, and — most of all — the level of care a resident needs, so the right way to read any figure is as a range, not a sticker. The numbers below are market estimates from named sources, meant to help you plan. They are not a quote for any one suite.

Calgary cost ranges — market estimates (2026)

  1. United Active Living suites — roughly $2,900–$9,100/month. UAL publishes suite pricing that varies by community, suite size, and the wellness and care options chosen.1 (UAL's own materials describe a broadly similar wide range across its communities.)
  2. Calgary memory care — about $3,500–$8,000/month (estimate). Specialized memory care in Calgary commonly falls in this range, with the figure driven by the level of supervision and support required.2
  3. Publicly funded continuing care — a set accommodation charge, not a market price. In Type A/B continuing care, the resident pays a government-set accommodation charge for room and meals while the care itself is publicly funded — a maximum of roughly $2,118/month for a shared room or $2,448/month for a private room (rates effective April 1, 2026).3
⚑ Important: private retirement living is private pay

Don't assume senior living is publicly funded by default. Most private retirement and independent living in Calgary — and much of assisted living — is private pay: you cover the monthly fee from your own funds. Only Alberta's publicly funded continuing care (Type A/B), accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment, has its care costs covered by the province, and even then the resident pays an accommodation charge.3 Knowing which bucket a community sits in is the first step to budgeting honestly.

What are the ways Alberta families pay?

Here is the reassuring part. Very few families write a single cheque from one account. Most assemble the monthly fee from a combination of the sources below — and some, like Veterans Affairs benefits, are missed simply because no one mentions them. Read these as options to explore, not advice; your own eligibility depends on your specific situation.

Common ways Alberta families pay

  1. Private savings and pensions. Retirement savings, workplace and government pensions, and investment income — the most direct source for many families.
  2. Sale or rental of the family home. Often the largest single source; selling frees the equity, while renting can create monthly income to offset care.
  3. Publicly funded continuing care (Type B / Designated Supportive Living). Where a parent qualifies through an Alberta Health Services assessment, AHS funds the care and the resident pays only a government-set accommodation charge for room, meals, and recreation.3
  4. The Alberta Seniors Benefit. An income-tested monthly benefit from the Government of Alberta for eligible lower-income seniors, which can help offset accommodation costs.4
  5. Veterans Affairs Canada. If your parent is an eligible veteran, Veterans Affairs Canada programs may help with the cost of care — a source families often overlook.5
✓ A note on eligibility

Each program has its own rules, and they change. Before counting on any source above, verify your family's eligibility with the right authority — Alberta Health Services for a continuing-care assessment, the Government of Alberta for the Alberta Seniors Benefit, Veterans Affairs Canada for veterans' programs, or a qualified financial advisor. Think of this as "talk to a professional," not financial advice — the right guide can often uncover help you didn't know you qualified for.

Does the government pay for senior living in Alberta?

Partly — and only for publicly funded continuing care, so let's be precise. In Alberta's publicly funded Type A and Type B continuing care homes (Type B was formerly called Designated Supportive Living), the health-care goods and services are publicly funded at no cost to the resident. What the resident pays is an accommodation charge — covering room, meals, and recreation — whose maximum is set by the Government of Alberta, not by the operator.3 Access to these publicly funded options runs through an Alberta Health Services assessment of care needs.

The government pays for the care in publicly funded continuing care — the resident pays a province-set charge for the room and meals. Private retirement living is a different world: it's private pay.

— How Alberta's continuing care funding actually works

There is an important protection built into this system. As of January 2026, residents who are eligible for seniors' financial benefits are assured at least $373 per month in disposable income after their accommodation charge is paid — a floor topped up through the Alberta Seniors Benefit if needed, so a resident isn't left without spending money of their own.3,4

The flip side is just as important to understand: private retirement and independent living — much of the senior-living market, including much of what United Active Living offers — is private pay. The province does not fund those monthly fees. That's not a catch; it's simply how Alberta's system is structured, and knowing it up front lets you plan with clear eyes. If a publicly funded option may fit your parent's needs and budget, the right first step is an Alberta Health Services assessment.
