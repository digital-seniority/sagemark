Independent vs Assisted vs Memory Care in Alberta: A Plain-Language Guide

If you're trying to figure out which kind of senior living your mom or dad actually needs, the labels can blur together. This guide explains independent living, assisted living, United Active Living's integrated model, and memory care (United Minds) in plain language — how they differ, how they map to Alberta's Type A/B and SL1–SL4 system, and how to tell which one fits your parent.

If you've started looking into senior living for a parent, you've probably met a wall of labels — independent, assisted, supportive, integrated, memory care — that all sound vaguely similar and never quite tell you which one is yours. Take a breath. The differences are actually straightforward once someone explains them in plain language, and you don't have to choose perfectly today. The single most useful idea to hold onto is this: the levels differ mainly by how much daily help and supervision they provide, from "very little" to "around the clock." This guide walks through each one, shows how they line up with Alberta's continuing care system, and gives you a calm way to tell which fits your parent right now.

Key takeaways

  • The levels of senior living differ mainly by how much daily help and supervision they provide — from independent living (very little) to memory care (a secure, specialized, around-the-clock setting).
  • Integrated living is United Active Living's blended model, so care can increase quietly over time without a disruptive move, and couples with different needs can stay together.

What are the levels of senior living?

Think of the levels as a gentle staircase, not a cliff. At one end are seniors who are fully capable and simply want an easier, more social way to live. At the other are people who need a secure, specialized setting and skilled help throughout the day. Most families are somewhere in the middle, and most parents move up a step or two over the years rather than all at once. The four levels you'll hear about most at United Active Living are independent living, assisted living, integrated living, and memory care (branded United Minds). The comparison table below is the fastest way to see them side by side; the sections that follow explain each one in human terms.

Independent living — who it's for

Independent living is for a parent who is, by and large, doing fine — but for whom home has started to feel like more work and less life. They can cook, drive, and manage their own day, yet the house is too big, the stairs are a worry, the yard is a chore, and the social world has quietly shrunk. Independent living trades all of that upkeep for prepared meals, housekeeping, a calendar full of things to do, and neighbours close by. There's no hands-on personal care in independent living; the value is convenience, connection, and the quiet safety of not being alone. At United Active Living, that means wellness programs, arts and music, and lifelong learning woven into ordinary days.

◇ A common misconception

Many adult children assume a move to a community means "giving up independence." For most parents it's the opposite — handing off the grocery run, the snow shovelling, and the cooking frees up energy for the things they actually enjoy. Independence is about living the day you want, not doing every chore yourself.

Assisted living — who it's for

Assisted living is the right fit when your parent is still very much themselves but needs regular, hands-on help with the tasks of daily living — bathing, dressing, grooming, managing medications, and moving safely around their space. It's not nursing-home care, and it isn't a loss of dignity; it's a team quietly handling the parts of the day that have become hard, so the rest of life can stay enjoyable. A good sign that assisted living fits is a pattern: missed medications, a fall or two, weight loss, or a parent who can no longer manage a shower safely on their own. If you're seeing that pattern, our guide to the signs it's time can help you read it. Assisted living keeps meals, housekeeping, and activities, and adds the personal care on top.

Integrated living — United Active Living's blended model

Most operators sort residents into separate buildings or wings by care level, which means a parent whose needs grow has to move — often the hardest, most disruptive part of the whole journey. Integrated living is United Active Living's answer to that problem. Rather than segregating people by how much help they need, an integrated community supports a mix of independent and assisted-living residents together, and care can be added quietly over time, in place, without a jarring transfer to a different setting.

For families, this matters in two very human ways. First, a parent can age in place within the same familiar hallways, the same friends, the same staff who know them. Second, a couple with different needs — one independent, one needing more help — can stay together rather than being split between buildings. It's a model built around continuity, which is exactly what most families are quietly hoping for when they start this search.

The hardest part of senior living is rarely the first move — it's the second one. Integrated living is designed so there usually isn't a second move.

Memory care (United Minds) — who it's for

Memory care is a secure, specialized setting for people living with Alzheimer's disease or another dementia, where the environment itself, the daily rhythm, and the staff are all designed around memory loss. It's the right level when confusion, disorientation, wandering, or safety risks mean that even attentive assisted living is no longer enough. Dementia is common and growing — roughly 1 in 10 Canadians over 65 lives with dementia4 — so if this is your family's path, you are far from alone. United Active Living delivers memory care through its United Minds approach: dementia-trained staff, a calm and secured environment that allows freedom of movement while preventing wandering, and around-the-clock supervision. The goal isn't only safety; it's a meaningful, dignified day for someone whose world has narrowed.

✓ Memory care is care, not confinement

A "secured" memory care setting can sound frightening to families. In practice it means a thoughtfully designed space where your parent can move freely and safely — gardens, walking loops, familiar cues — without the risk of getting lost. Good memory care expands a person's safe world; it doesn't shrink it.

How this maps to Alberta's system

Here is where the language gets technical, so let's keep it plain. Alberta describes care needs in supportive living levels SL1 to SL4. Roughly speaking, SL1 is private retirement and assisted-living apartments for fairly independent seniors; SL2 is seniors lodges; and SL3 and SL4 are for higher needs, with SL4 and SL4-Dementia providing 24-hour on-site nursing for people who need the most support — including secure dementia care.2 That's the same staircase described above, just with Alberta's labels.

Separately, Alberta's publicly funded continuing care homes are now classed as Type A and Type B, where Type B was formerly called Designated Supportive Living (DSL).1 In these publicly funded homes, the health care goods and services are publicly funded at no cost; what residents pay is an accommodation charge for their room, meals, and recreation, and the maximum for that charge is set by the Government of Alberta.1 As a low-income protection, the province assures eligible residents at least $373 per month in disposable income after the accommodation charge is paid (as of January 2026).1

⚑ Private pay vs publicly funded — the key distinction

Private retirement and independent living, and most private assisted living (which is much of what United Active Living offers), is private pay — funded from savings, pensions, or the sale or rental of a home. Publicly funded supportive living (Type B / Designated Supportive Living) is different: it's accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment of your parent's care needs, not simply by choosing it.3 If publicly funded care matters to your family, start by asking Alberta Health Services for an assessment.

The honest takeaway: the level your parent needs (based on care) and the way you pay for it (private vs publicly funded) are two separate questions. For a closer look at the dollars, see our companion guide to the cost of senior living in Calgary.

How to tell which one your parent needs

The most reassuring thing to know is that you usually don't decide this from a single bad day — you read a pattern over time. Use the quick check below as a starting point, then let a tour and a care assessment confirm it. For publicly funded options, an Alberta Health Services assessment is the formal step that determines the level your parent qualifies for.

A quick way to tell which level fits

  1. Independent living — your parent is safe and capable, but lonely, tired of upkeep, or rattling around a house that's too big.
  2. Assisted living — they need regular hands-on help with bathing, dressing, medications, or mobility, and you're seeing missed meds, falls, or weight loss.
  3. Integrated living — you want a parent to age in place without a future move, or a couple with different needs who want to stay together.
  4. Memory care (United Minds) — memory loss is causing confusion, wandering, or safety risks that a general setting can't safely manage.

If you're still not sure where your parent lands, that's completely normal — and it's exactly the conversation our team has every day. A short tour and a care assessment will usually replace a frightening unknown with a clear, workable picture. Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between independent living, assisted living, and memory care? +

Independent living is for seniors who can manage daily life on their own but want the convenience, social life, and safety of a community. Assisted living adds hands-on help with everyday tasks such as bathing, dressing, medications, and getting around. Memory care is a secure, specialized setting for people living with Alzheimer's disease or another dementia who need a calm, structured environment and dementia-trained staff. The main difference is the level of daily support and supervision each one provides.

How do these levels of care map to Alberta's system? +

Private retirement and independent living roughly correspond to supportive living level 1 (SL1) and seniors lodges (SL2). Assisted living covers the middle SL levels. Higher-needs and secure dementia care correspond to SL4 and SL4-Dementia, which provide 24-hour on-site care. Alberta's publicly funded continuing care homes are now classed as Type A and Type B (Type B was formerly called Designated Supportive Living). Much of private retirement and assisted living is private pay, while publicly funded Type B / Designated Supportive Living is accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment.

What is integrated living at United Active Living? +

Integrated living is United Active Living's blended model. Instead of sorting residents into separate buildings by care level, a community supports a mix of independent and assisted-living residents together, and care can increase quietly over time without a disruptive move. It is designed so a couple with different needs can stay together, and so a parent can age in place within the same familiar community as their needs change.

How do I know which level of care my parent needs? +

Watch for a pattern over time rather than a single bad day. If your parent is safe and capable but lonely or tired of upkeep, independent or integrated living may fit. If they need regular hands-on help with bathing, dressing, medications, or mobility, that points to assisted living. If memory loss is causing confusion, wandering, or safety risks, memory care may be the safer setting. A tour and a care assessment — and, for publicly funded options, an Alberta Health Services assessment — will help confirm the right fit.

Does Alberta Health Services pay for assisted living and memory care? +

It depends on the setting. In Alberta's publicly funded Type A and Type B continuing care homes, health care goods and services are publicly funded at no cost, while residents pay an accommodation charge for their room, meals, and recreation, with a maximum set by the Government of Alberta. Private retirement and most private assisted living is private pay. Publicly funded supportive living (Type B / Designated Supportive Living) is accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment of care needs.
