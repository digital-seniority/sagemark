About senior living

What is senior living? +

Senior living is a broad term for housing and care options designed for older adults — from independent living apartments for active seniors who simply want a maintenance-free, social lifestyle, through assisted living for those who need a hand with daily tasks, to memory care for people living with dementia. At United Active Living, our communities bring these options together around a shared philosophy of wellness, creative expression, and lifelong learning, so a resident can move between levels of support without leaving the community they call home. Our guide to senior living in Calgary is the best place to start.

What's the difference between independent, assisted, and memory care? +

Independent living is for active seniors who want a private suite, prepared meals, housekeeping, and a full social calendar without the work of running a home. Assisted living adds personal support — help with bathing, dressing, medications, and mobility — for people who need a hand with daily tasks but are not in crisis. Memory care (our United Minds program) is purpose-built for people living with dementia, with specially trained staff, a secure and calming environment, and structured days. We also offer integrated living, where these levels blend so couples and neighbours with different needs can stay together. We compare them in detail in our guide to the levels of care in Alberta.

Is assisted living the same as a nursing home or long-term care? +

No. In Alberta, a nursing home (facility-based continuing care, sometimes called long-term care) provides 24-hour on-site nursing for people with complex, ongoing medical needs and is part of the publicly funded continuing care system.1 Assisted living and retirement living are residential, home-like settings focused on day-to-day support — meals, housekeeping, help with personal care, and social life — rather than hospital-level medical treatment. United Active Living's communities are retirement and supportive living communities, not nursing homes; when a resident's medical needs grow beyond what we provide, we help families navigate the next step with Alberta Health Services.

Who is memory care (United Minds) for? +

United Minds, our memory care program, is for adults living with Alzheimer's disease or another form of dementia whose needs have grown beyond what can be safely managed at home or in independent or assisted living. It often becomes the right fit when wandering, confusion, safety incidents, or the demands of around-the-clock supervision begin to overwhelm family caregivers. United Minds pairs a secure, calming environment with dementia-trained staff and the arts, music, and engagement that run through everything United Active Living does — supporting both the resident and the family.

How do I know when it's time? +

It is rarely one single moment — it is usually a pattern that builds over time. Common signs include increasing forgetfulness or missed medications, unexplained weight loss or an empty fridge, falls or near-falls, growing isolation, a home that is no longer being kept up, and a family caregiver who is exhausted. If you are noticing several of these together, it may be time to look at options. Our guide on the signs it's time walks through them in detail, and our team is always glad to talk through what you're seeing.

Costs & Alberta funding

How much does senior living cost in Calgary? +

It depends on the community, the suite, and the level of care, so the honest answer is to call for a personalized quote rather than rely on a single number. As a guide, United Active Living publishes suite pricing that ranges roughly from $2,900 to $9,100 per month depending on the community and level of support,2 and independent Calgary market sources estimate memory care in the city at roughly $3,500 to $8,000 per month.3 These are market estimates, not a fixed price — what your family pays reflects your loved one's actual needs and the suite you choose. Our guide on the cost of senior living in Calgary breaks it down further.

Does the Alberta government pay for senior living? +

Sometimes — it depends on the type of care. Alberta's publicly funded continuing care system includes Type A and Type B continuing care homes (Type B was formerly called Designated Supportive Living).4 In those settings, health care goods and services are publicly funded at no cost, while residents pay an accommodation charge for their room, meals, and recreation, with the maximum set by the Government of Alberta.5 Access to publicly funded supportive living is determined through an Alberta Health Services assessment. Private retirement and independent living — which describes much of what United Active Living offers — is generally private pay rather than publicly funded. As of January 2026, residents who qualify for seniors' financial benefits are assured at least $373 per month in disposable income after the accommodation charge.5

How do families pay? +

Most families combine more than one source. Common ones in Alberta include private savings and pensions, the sale or rental of a home, the income-tested Alberta Seniors Benefit, and — for publicly funded Type B supportive living accessed through an Alberta Health Services assessment — public funding of care with a resident accommodation charge. Veterans Affairs Canada benefits may apply for those who qualify. Because every family's situation is different, the simplest next step is a short conversation so we can explain which options may fit. Our guide on the cost of senior living in Calgary explains how these pieces work together.

Safety & daily life

How do you keep residents safe? +

Safety is built into the design of every United Active Living community. Suites and common areas are built for accessibility and fall prevention, team members are on site around the clock, and our memory care neighbourhoods (United Minds) have secure entrances and enclosed outdoor spaces so residents can enjoy fresh air without the risk of wandering. Our aim is a setting that is genuinely secure without ever feeling like a lockdown — calm, welcoming, and reassuring for residents and families alike.

What does a typical day look like? +

Days at United Active Living are built around wellness, creative expression, and lifelong learning rather than just a schedule of meals. A typical day might include a morning fitness or movement class, an art studio or music session, a lecture or discussion group, time in shared gardens and lounges, and unhurried meals with neighbours. Activities are shaped around residents' own histories and interests, and families are always welcome to take part. The point is a full, engaged life — not simply care.

Can my parent stay as their needs change (age in place)? +

In most cases, yes. United Active Living's integrated living model is designed so residents can move between independent living, assisted living, and memory care as their needs change — often without leaving the community, the staff, and the neighbours they know. That continuity spares families another disruptive move and lets couples with different needs stay close. Because every person's journey is different, we'll talk with you honestly about your loved one's needs and how we can meet them over time.

About United Active Living

Where are your communities? +

United Active Living operates six communities across Calgary: Fish Creek North, Fish Creek West, Garrison Green, Lake Bonavista Village, The Lodge at Valley Ridge, and Trinity Lodge. Each offers a mix of independent living, assisted living, integrated living, and memory care, so families can find a community in the part of the city that suits them. What makes United Active Living different? +

United Active Living is built around a wellness and creative-expression model rather than a traditional care-first one. The arts, music, lifelong learning, fitness, and genuine community are woven into daily life, and our integrated living approach lets residents age in place as their needs change. Our memory care program, United Minds, brings that same philosophy to dementia care. The result is a community where older adults are encouraged to keep growing, creating, and connecting — not simply to be looked after.
