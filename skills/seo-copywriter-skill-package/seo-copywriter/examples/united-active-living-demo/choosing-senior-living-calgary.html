Choosing a Senior-Living Community in Calgary: 12 Questions to Ask on a Tour

A nice lobby tells you very little. What tells you everything is how a community answers hard questions — and what you see when you watch ordinary life unfold. This guide gives you the 12 questions to ask on every tour, the green and red flags to read, and a simple way to compare your options once the tours are done.

United Active Living Care Team

⚡ Quick answer

To choose well, judge a community on three things: the answers to your questions, what you observe with your own senses, and how the team treats the residents who already live there. Ask about staffing day and overnight, training and turnover, safety, personalized care plans, ageing in place, a typical day, dining, medications, family communication, all-in cost, Alberta licensing, and trial stays. Then visit at least twice — once on a booked tour and once unannounced or at a mealtime — because two visits almost always beat one. The community that answers your hard questions directly, and looks calm and engaged when no one's performing, is the one worth shortlisting.

By the time you're booking tours, you've usually done the hardest part — you've accepted that home may no longer be enough, and you're ready to find the right place. Take a breath. This is the stage where worry can quietly turn into a decision you feel good about, because for the first time you're not reading about senior living in the abstract — you're walking through real communities and meeting the people who would care for your parent.

The trouble is that tours are designed to impress. A bright lobby, fresh flowers, and a warm greeting are easy to arrange for an hour. What you actually need to know is harder to stage: how the community runs on an ordinary Tuesday afternoon, how it handles a fall at 3 a.m., and whether the staff who smiled at you will still be there in a year. This guide gives you the questions that get past the lobby — and the things to watch for that no brochure will tell you. If you'd like a one-page version to carry with you, our printable tour checklist puts every question on a single sheet.

Key takeaways

  • Judge a community on three things together — its answers, what you observe, and how it treats current residents — not on the look of the lobby.
  • The 12 questions below cover the things that actually predict quality: staffing, training, safety, care planning, ageing in place, daily life, dining, medications, communication, cost, licensing, and trial stays.

How do I evaluate a senior-living community?

The most reliable way to evaluate a community is to stop trying to grade the building and start reading the care it delivers every day. A beautiful suite matters far less than whether a call bell is answered quickly at night, whether your parent's care plan is reviewed when their needs change, and whether the staff genuinely know the people they look after. So bring three lenses to every tour.

First, the answers. Ask direct questions and listen for direct answers. Confident, specific responses — actual staffing numbers, a clear policy, a named process — are a good sign. Vague, deflecting, or over-rehearsed answers are worth noting. Second, your own observation. Tours engage your eyes and ears for a reason: a calm, clean, engaged community feels different from a tense or idle one, and you will sense it. Third, how current residents are treated. The way a staff member speaks to a resident when they think no one important is watching tells you more than any sales pitch. If you're still deciding which level of care your parent needs, settle that first — it shapes every question below.

The 12 questions to ask on every tour

Print these, bring them, and ask every community the same set so you can compare like with like. There are no wrong answers in isolation — what you're listening for is specificity and consistency. Note how each community responds, and watch for the ones that answer hard questions plainly rather than steering you back to the amenities.

The 12 questions to ask on every tour

  1. What are your staffing ratios — daytime and overnight? Care doesn't pause after dark. Ask for the actual ratio of care staff to residents during the day and on the overnight shift, and how that changes if someone needs more help.
  2. How are your staff trained, and what is your staff turnover like? Ask about dementia and personal-care training, and how long care staff typically stay. Familiar faces are a quiet but powerful sign of a healthy community.
  3. How do you keep residents safe — from wandering, falls, and emergencies? Ask about secured areas for memory care, fall-prevention practices, call-bell response times, and what happens in a night-time emergency.
  4. Does each resident have a personalized care plan, and how often is it reviewed? Good care is individual. Ask who writes the plan, how the family is involved, and what triggers a review when needs change.
  5. Can my parent age in place as their needs change? A move is hard; a second move is harder. Ask whether the community offers a range of care — from independent and assisted living through to memory care — so a rising level of need doesn't mean starting over somewhere new.
  6. What does a typical day look like, and what wellness programming do you offer? Ask to see a real activity calendar. Look for genuine engagement — movement, music, the arts, lifelong learning — not just a television in a common room.
  7. Tell me about dining. Ask to see a menu, ask about choice and dietary needs, and if you can, stay for a meal. Dining is one of the truest tests of daily quality of life.
  8. How are medications and medical coordination handled? Ask who administers medications, how they coordinate with a resident's doctor and pharmacy, and how they manage a change in health.
  9. How will you communicate with our family? Ask how and how often they share updates, who the family's main contact is, and how quickly they reach you if something changes.
  10. What is the all-in monthly cost, and what triggers an increase? Ask what the base fee includes, what is billed separately, how care-level increases are priced, and how often fees rise. Get the all-in picture, not the headline number.
  11. What licensing, standards, or accreditation are you held to? Ask whether the community is a publicly funded Alberta continuing care home (Type A or Type B) or a private residence, and request recent inspection or accreditation results.1
  12. Do you offer a trial or respite stay? A short respite stay lets your parent experience the community before a permanent move — one of the best ways to be sure before you commit.
✓ Two visits beat one

The single most useful thing you can do is visit twice. Book the first visit as a proper tour so you can ask all 12 questions. Then come back a second time unannounced, or at a mealtime — without an appointment, when no tour is being staged for you. The contrast between the two visits is where the truth lives: a community that feels just as calm, warm, and engaged on the unannounced visit is one you can trust.

Green flags vs red flags

Once you've asked your questions, much of what's left is reading the room. The table below pairs the encouraging signs against the warning signs for the same moments of a tour. Remember that this is about patterns: a single ringing call bell during a busy lunch is human, but a community where bells go unanswered and answers stay vague is telling you something. Trust the pattern.

Green flags — encouraging signs Red flags — warning signs
Staff greet residents warmly and by name Staff walk past residents without acknowledging them
Residents look engaged — in activities, conversation, or contentedly occupied Residents seem bored, isolated, or parked in front of a television
Common areas are calm, clean, and pleasant-smelling A persistent odour, clutter, or an unusually tense atmosphere
Call bells are answered promptly Call bells ringing unanswered for long stretches
Clear, direct answers on staffing, cost, and safety Evasive or vague answers, especially about cost and ratios
Long-tenured staff and low turnover High turnover, or staff who can't speak to how long they've been there
Families are welcomed to visit anytime and stay for a meal Visits feel restricted, scripted, or only by appointment

Trust what you observe

It's easy to second-guess a gut feeling on a day this important, but your observations are real data — so trust what you observe. The best communities don't need a performance, because the everyday version of themselves is the selling point. That's exactly why an unannounced visit, or one timed to a mealtime, is so revealing: you see the place as your parent would actually live in it.

The lobby is what a community wants you to see. A Tuesday afternoon — call bells, the dining room, the look on residents' faces — is what your parent will actually live in. Choose for the Tuesday afternoon.

Watch the small, unscripted things. Do staff make eye contact with residents and speak to them as adults? Are residents dressed, groomed, and out of their rooms? Is there real warmth between the team and the people they care for? This is also where a community's difference shows: United Active Living's six Calgary communities are built around wellness, creative expression, and lifelong learning — so on an ordinary afternoon you're as likely to find an art studio, a music session, or a class in progress as a quiet lounge. That daily texture of engagement is one of the things you can only judge by being there.

After the tour — comparing your options

When the tours are done, resist the urge to decide from memory — impressions blur quickly. Instead, compare your options on paper. Lay your shortlisted communities side by side and score each one on the same things you asked about: staffing, safety, care planning, ageing in place, daily life, dining, communication, all-in cost, and your overall feel of the place. A simple grid turns three good-but-fuzzy visits into a clear-eyed comparison.

Two questions usually settle it. First, which community can care for my parent not just today, but as their needs change? — because the ability to age in place can spare your family a second wrenching move. Second, which place felt most like somewhere my parent could genuinely live well? The numbers narrow the field; that human answer usually makes the choice. Frequently asked questions

How do I evaluate a senior-living community on a tour? +

Evaluate a community on three things together: the answers to your questions, what you observe with your own senses, and how the team treats current residents. Ask about staffing day and overnight, training and turnover, safety, personalized care plans, and all-in cost — then watch the small things, like whether staff greet residents by name and how the dining room feels. Visit at least twice, with one visit unannounced or at a mealtime, so you see ordinary life rather than a staged tour.

What are the most important questions to ask on a senior-living tour? +

The most important questions are about staffing levels day and overnight, staff training and turnover, how the community keeps residents safe from wandering and falls, whether each resident has a personalized care plan, whether your parent can age in place as needs change, what a typical day looks like, how dining and medications are handled, how the team communicates with families, the all-in monthly cost and what triggers an increase, the community's Alberta licensing or accreditation, and whether a trial or respite stay is possible.

How many times should I visit a community before deciding? +

Plan to visit at least twice. Make one visit a scheduled tour where you can ask your questions, and make a second visit unannounced or at a mealtime, when you'll see ordinary daily life rather than a prepared presentation. Two visits almost always tell you more than one — the second visit is where you confirm that what you were told matches what you see.

Are senior-living communities in Alberta licensed or inspected? +

It depends on the type of community. Publicly funded Type A and Type B continuing care homes (Type B was formerly Designated Supportive Living) operate under Government of Alberta standards and are monitored through Alberta Health Services. Private retirement and independent-living residences are not licensed the same way, though many participate in voluntary accreditation. Ask each community directly which standards it is held to, and request to see recent inspection or accreditation results.

What is the difference between a green flag and a red flag on a tour? +

A green flag is a sign the community is genuinely well-run — staff greeting residents by name, residents engaged in activities, calm and clean common areas, and clear, direct answers to hard questions. A red flag is a warning sign — call bells ringing unanswered, a strong odour, residents who seem bored or isolated, evasive answers about cost or staffing, and high staff turnover. One small red flag may be nothing, but a pattern of them is a reason to keep looking.
